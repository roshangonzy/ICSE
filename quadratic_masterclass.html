Quadratic Equations

Master the standard form, factorization, the quadratic formula, and the nature of roots to secure full marks in Section A & B of your ICSE board exam.

1 The Standard Form

A quadratic equation is a polynomial equation of degree 2. This means the highest power of the variable $x$ is exactly 2. To solve any quadratic equation, you must always arrange it into the Standard Form first:

$$ax^2 + bx + c = 0$$

The Golden Rule:

$a, b,$ and $c$ are real numbers, but $a$ can never be zero ($a \neq 0$). If $a$ becomes zero, the $x^2$ term disappears, and the equation downgrades into a simple linear equation ($bx + c = 0$).

2 Solving by Factorisation

Factorisation relies on the Zero Product Property. If the product of two brackets is zero, then at least one of the brackets must be equal to zero.

If $(x - p)(x - q) = 0$, then either $x = p$ or $x = q$.

Example: Solve $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$

  • Step 1: Find two numbers that multiply to $+6$ (the 'c' term) and add to $-5$ (the 'b' term). Those numbers are $-2$ and $-3$.
  • Step 2: Split the middle term:
    $x^2 - 2x - 3x + 6 = 0$
  • Step 3: Factorize by grouping:
    $x(x - 2) - 3(x - 2) = 0$
  • Step 4: Extract common bracket:
    $(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0$
  • Step 5: Apply Zero Product Property:
    $x = 2$ or $x = 3$

3 The Quadratic Formula

Sometimes equations cannot be factorised easily (for example, if the roots are decimals or irrational numbers like $\sqrt{3}$). In this case, we use Shridharacharya's Formula:

$$x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}$$

The $\pm$ symbol indicates that you will get two answers: one by adding the square root, and one by subtracting it.

4 Nature of Roots (The Discriminant)

You don't always need to solve the entire formula to know what kind of answers you'll get. The expression under the square root, $b^2 - 4ac$, is called the Discriminant ($D$). It completely determines the nature of the roots.

D > 0

Real & Distinct

The equation has two different real answers. (e.g., $x=2$ and $x=5$).

D = 0

Real & Equal

The equation has one repeated real answer. The expression is a perfect square.

D < 0

Imaginary Roots

No real roots exist because you cannot take the square root of a negative number.
